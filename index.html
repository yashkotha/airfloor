    Reading the numbers

    The WHO says yearly PM2.5 should stay under 5 µg/m³. Most Indian cities sit well above that, so the goal is not perfect air, it is choosing the floor that gives you the cleanest slice of what is available.

    Elevation, sea level to rooftop

    Your building is a thin sliver on top of the land. That is why oxygen barely changes with floor. The pollution that matters lives in the first 150 m above the street.

    How the score works

    Each floor's height feeds five curves. The overall score is their weighted sum. PM2.5 carries the most weight because it is the one thing that genuinely changes your health here. Oxygen carries almost none.

    35%
    PM2.5 / inversion
    Climbs with height as floors clear the trapped night air near the ground.
    20%
    Street fumes
    CO, NO₂ and diesel soot fall away fast in the first 50 m, then flatten out.
    20%
    Ventilation
    Best around the mid floors. Up high, wind forces sealed windows and full-time AC.
    20%
    Liveability
    Fewer mosquitoes and less noise with height, but harder escape and more sway.
    5%
    Oxygen
    Almost flat across any single building. Small weight on purpose, it barely matters.
    Honest limits

    The score is a clear guide, not a certified standard. Elevation and live air are real and sourced below. The shape of the height curves comes from standard atmospheric physics tuned to your location's pollution level. The biggest unknown is how deep the night inversion sits, which shifts the best floor by a few floors, never the overall story.

    What it means for you

    Best value

    Aim for a band, not one magic floor

    The score is nearly flat across the sweet-spot band. Any home in it clears the street fumes, beats most inversion nights, and still lets you open a window. Buy anywhere in the band and you have already won.

    Worth knowing

    The very top loses on wind, not oxygen

    The highest floor has the cleanest raw air but the worst airflow, plus harder escape, sway and sun load. Its penalty is about daily life, not thin air. Oxygen is a rounding error in any building.

    Weakest spot

    The ground floor loses where it counts

    The lowest floors sit inside both the street fumes and the trapped night air at the exact hours you sleep and breathe deepest. No view or garden makes up for the dirtiest slice of air.

    Bottom line

    Pick a home in the sweet-spot band, facing away from the busiest road, confirm the windows actually open, and run a HEPA purifier in each bedroom. That beats chasing the highest floor you can afford.

    Before you buy, ask

    The score points you to the right floors. These questions and papers help you weigh the building behind them. Carry this list to the site visit.

    1. 1
      Distance from the roadHow many metres set the tower back from the nearest busy road, and which way do the bedrooms face?
    2. 2
      Generator and basement exhaustWhere do the diesel generator and car-park exhausts vent, and which floors sit in their path?
    3. 3
      Cross ventilationIs every flat drawn for cross ventilation, or do some rooms open to only one side?
    4. 4
      Sewage plant locationWhere is the sewage treatment plant, and how far is it from the lowest homes?
    5. 5
      Windows that openOn the floor you are considering, do the windows truly open, or is the tower sealed for full-time air conditioning?
    6. 6
      What sits within a kilometreIs there a highway, a factory, a landfill, or a years-long construction site close by?
    7. 7
      What comes up nextWhat is approved for the empty plots around this project over the next five years?
    8. 8
      Fresh-air upkeepIf higher floors rely on mechanical fresh-air units, who changes the filters and how often?
    1. 1
      Sanctioned building planThe plan approved by the local authority, showing the tower's real position, height and setbacks.
    2. 2
      Occupancy CertificateThe OC confirms the building is legally fit to live in; do not take possession without it.
    3. 3
      RERA registrationThe project's RERA number and page, where approvals, timelines and complaints stay on public record.
    4. 4
      Pollution board consentThe State Pollution Control Board consent to operate the diesel generators and the sewage plant.
    5. 5
      Environmental clearanceFor larger projects, the clearance and its conditions on air, dust and green cover.
    6. 6
      Fire safety NOCThe fire department no-objection certificate for the tower's height and its escape routes.
    7. 7
      Sewage plant test reportsRecent reports showing the plant meets discharge norms, so odour and fumes stay in check.
    8. 8
      Structural stability certificateProof of structural soundness, which matters most for a resale or an older building.

    Sources and accuracy

    Every live number on this page is fetched at the moment you search, from named scientific sources, and time-stamped. No number is invented or cached from memory.

    Why you can trust these

    Air quality comes from the Copernicus Atmosphere Monitoring Service, a physics-based model run by the EU, not a network of cheap sensors that can drift or be gamed. Elevation comes from the Copernicus 90 m digital terrain model. PIN codes resolve through the official India Post directory. Where a single low-cost station disagrees, treat the model as the steadier reference and re-check on a second day.
